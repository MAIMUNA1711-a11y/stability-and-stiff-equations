Why it matters: steps needed for a stable, accurate run

Explicit (Forward Euler) — stability-limited Implicit (Backward Euler) — accuracy-limited

An explicit method must keep h ≤ 2/|λ_max| for stability the entire run, even though the slow mode needs the simulation to keep going for ~5/|λ_min| — that mismatch is where the huge step count comes from. An implicit method skips it entirely by choosing h from accuracy alone.

Eigenvalues on the complex plane

λ_max (fast) λ_min (slow)

Both eigenvalues sit on the real axis (no oscillation). λ_max sits far to the left of λ_min — that distance on the plane is the stiffness.

Notes & limitations of this tool ▾
  • Linear stability only. The Stability Region Explorer, Solution Comparison, and both practice problems analyze the linear test equation dy/dt = λy. Real nonlinear systems have state- and time-varying local eigenvalues, so this is a deliberate simplification for teaching the core idea.
  • Practice Problem 2's recommendation is a heuristic. It classifies purely on the stiffness ratio S = |λ_max|/|λ_min| against the S<10 / 10–1000 / >1000 thresholds. Real solver selection also weighs accuracy requirements, error tolerance, the system's Jacobian, and nonlinear behavior.
  • No adaptive step size. Every h on this page is fixed for the whole run. Production solvers (e.g. ode45, LSODA) adjust h automatically from a local error estimate — that adaptive logic isn't modeled here.
  • Implicit solves are shown as a formula, not a solver. Backward Euler and Trapezoidal are evaluated via their closed-form amplification factor R(z). In practice, each implicit step on a nonlinear system needs an iterative solve (typically Newton's method) that isn't visualized.
  • Complex eigenvalues are partially supported. The Stability Region Explorer lets Im(λ) move the point z=hλ off the real axis.
